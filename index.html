Intraindividual Variability of Sleep: An Ongoing Systematic Review

Features of an individual's sleep/wake pattern, such as sleep timing, duration, and quality vary from night to night. Sleep across multiple days can be characterized along two dimensions: the individual mean?, and the intraindividual variability (IIV)?. This site is an ongoing effort to better understand sleep IIV, and provide information and tools for researchers to incorporate IIV as a second dimension in studying sleep/wake patterns.

This site builds on content of the first systematic review on the correlates of sleep/wake IIV, so that evidence synthesis could grow alongside the growth of new knowledge. The initial review can be accessed via open access below.

Bei, Wiley, Trinder, & Manber (2016). Beyond the mean: A systematic review on the correlates of daily intraindividual variability of sleep/wake patterns. Sleep Medicine Reviews, 28, 104-120.

On this website, you will find an ongoing systematic review on the correlates of sleep IIV, and information on methodologies in researching IIV. We try to update this site regularly. A major update using the same methodology as in the initial systematic review will occur annually in December/January. The initial review covered records up to November 2014, and identified 53 articles. The systematic updates in Jan 2016, 2017, 2018, 2019 added an additional 4, 13, 11, and 11 studies respectively, bringing the total number of eligible studies to 92 on this website. Those added in the most recent update are marked with * in the table. Between annual updates, we will also add new studies (marked with !) that are notified via search alerts or other miscellaneous sources.

A. Demographic and environmental factors (n = 27)

Age (n = 22): Overall, in community samples, younger age was associated with more variable self-report sleep timing, duration, and quality. Over a 10-y period, sleep timing IIV did not change significantly among middle aged or older adults who lived alone, but reduced among older adults who cohabitated.
Sex (n = 8): Findings on the association between sex and sleep/wake IIV were inconclusive.
Race/ethnicity (n = 8): These studies examined different samples. Overall, minority race was associated with more variable sleep quality, and findings on sleep timing and duration are mixed.
Cohabitation (n = 2): In mid-old aged community adults, cohabitation was associated with less variable sleep timing and duration.
Education (n = 4): 3 studies found non-significant association between sleep duration IIV and education; 1 found more variable SOL associated with lower education.
Light exposure (n = 2) is shown to be lower in individuals with higher IIV.
Care-giving (n=2) to dementia patients or family chaos during the first year of parenthood was associated with significantly more variable sleep patterns. Home environment was associated with more variable sleep duration a sleep laboratory. Shiftwork, weekend, but not morning commitments are associated with more variable sleep timing.

B. Physical health (n = 25)

Health conditions (n = 6): In older adults, more variable sleep timing and duration were associated with higher rates of and risk for diabetes, heart conditions, and poorer self-reported health. Patients with chronic kidney disease had significantly more variable sleep quality than healthy controls. Patients with Type 1 diabetes are more likely to have poorer glycaemic control if their sleep timing and duration. In a community sample, more variable sleep timing was also associated with risk for diabetes. The onset of a serious physical illness, however, did not contribute to changes in sleep timing over a 10-y period in middle-aged adults.
Body weight (n = 9): In older adults, more variable sleep timing and duration was associated with higher BMI and higher rates of obesity. Among young adults, more variable sleep timing was associated with greater weight gain starting university. One study found that these associations may be moderated by delay discounting and response disinhibition.
Childbearing (n = 2): Caring for a newborn is associated with more variable sleep timing, duration, and quality, with the highest IIV observed during the first postpartum week.
DLMO (n = 2): Higher sleep IIV associated with later DLMO; greater changes in DLMO associated with more variable SOL.
Biomarkers (n = 4): Higher inflammatory biomarkers (IL-6 and TNF-α) were linked to more variable sleep timing and duration in older adults, particularly among those without sleep complaints. Across menstrual cycles, higher mean estradiol has been associated with more variable sleep duration. More variable sleep timing and duration were associated with flatter cortisol diurnal slope, but associations between sleep IIV and multi-system physiological dysregulation (allostatic load) is non-significant after controlling for multiple covariates. In a community sample, more variable sleep duration was associated with greater total white blood cell and absolute neutrophil counts, but these were nonsignificant after adjusting for multiple comparisons. In older adults, more irregular sleeper had higher serum hemoglobin and fasting blood glucose.
Other: Greater pain reports are associated with more variable discrepancy in objectively measured and sleep-reported sleep duration and quality. Having visual blindness was associated with more variable sleep efficiency and sleep offset timing. VO₂peak was associated with sleep duration IIV, and exercise intervention reduces sleep IIV.

C. Psychological factors and function (n = 47)

Bipolar Disorder (n = 8): Compared to healthy controls, patients in remission from bipolar disorder had significantly more variable sleep duration and quality. Individuals at higher risk for bipolar disorder also reported more variable sleep timing, duration, and quality. Among Bipolar patients, more variable sleep duration and quality were associated with lower social rhythm regularity, greater symptom severity; more variable sleep duration was also linked to greater negative affect and greater variability in daily mood and affect.
Depressive symptoms (n = 13): There is evidence that in both community and clinical samples, depressive symptomatology is associated with more variable sleep duration, and quality. Findings on sleep timing is somewhat mixed.
Cognitive function (n = 10): Findings on cognitive function are inconclusive, with most studies focusing on different samples and cognitive outcomes. Recently, studies linked high sleep IIV to risk for mild cognitive impairment and lower GPA. Another study found more variable sleep duration to be associated with worse verbal learning and working memory in Bipolar Disorder. A 2018 study on sleep duration IIV and executive functioning had mixed findings.
Daytime consequence (n = 9): Sleep/wake IIV has been studied in relation to daytime fatigue, sleepiness, performance on psychomotor vigilance tasks (PVT), and microsleeps after sleep restriction, with mixed and inconclusive findings. There is some evidence that daytime consequences of, and the recovery from, sleep deprivation/disruption might be related to habitual sleep/wake IIV. More irregular sleep is linked to poorer academic performance.
Chronotype (n = 8): In both university students and insomnia patients, evening chronotype was associated with significantly more variable sleep timing, but not duration or quality. In community based study of older adults, eveningness was mildly associated with more irregular sleep. Chronotype was not shown to be associated with sleep IIV in samples of Bipolar or blind individuals, but in patients with schizophrenia, more variable sleep timing, duration, and quality were associated with more eveningness and delayed sleep phase.
Stress (n = 5): In community adults, more variable sleep duration was associated with higher number of stressful life events and social strain, but was not related to a biomarker of stress, norepinephrine, or social support. In young adults, sleep duration IIV was related to different patterns of adjustment during transition into college. In Bipolar patients, higher sleep duration IIV was associated with more variable daily stress report.
Other: Compared to healthy controls, individuals with comorbid ADHD and delayed sleep phase disorder had significantly more variable sleep onset latency; those with schizophrenia tended to have more variable sleep duration and quality; veterans with PTSD had more variable sleep duration and quality. With regards to personality, irregular sleepers were shown to be lower on dominance, sociability, self-acceptance, self-control, achievement via conformance, and intellectual efficiency, but higher on flexibility, compared to regular sleepers. High sleep IIV was also linked to higher suicidal ideation, more challenging behaviours in low-functioning autism, and weaker association between self-report and actigraphy measured sleep duration.

D. Insomnia and sleep complaints (n = 25)

Sleep complaints (n = 14): Compared to healthy controls and good sleepers, individuals with insomnia symptoms have consistently been shown to have greater IIV on variables related to sleep quality, such as sleep onset latency, sleep efficiency, nighttime awakenings, and self-report sleep quality. Differences in sleep timing and duration IIV between those with and without insomnia symptoms were less consistent.
Sleep interventions (n = 9): 6 of the 8 intervention studies used CBT-I, and reported overall reduced sleep/wake variability after intervention, especially when sleep was measured via self-report. Further, most results were maintained at follow-up. Findings have been mixed on the relevance of baseline or post-treatment sleep/wake IIV to insomnia treatment outcomes. A recent study showed that more variable sleep at baseline predicted better treatment response, and that treatment related changes in TSTv and SOLv was mediated by reductions in sleep timing and TIB IIV.

E. Experimentally manipulating IIV (n = 3)

  1. Bonnet et al. (1982) experimentally manipulated sleep schedules of 13 healthy university student. The manipulation imposed 38 d of regular sleep in the laboratory, followed by 4 wk habitual sleep at home (irregular condition). They found that the average body temperature was lower during regular versus irregular conditions, but participants did not have significantly different mood, cognitive performance, or sleep architecture.
  2. Manber et al. (1996) conducted a home-based experiment on 39 university students who were irregular sleepers and had self-reported daytime sleepiness. After 12 d baseline, participants were randomized into either regular sleep condition (i.e., sleep timing to remain within 1 h of habitual sleep timing) or sleep only condition (i.e., natural sleep timing without specific instruction); both groups were instructed to sleep for ≥7.5 h to avoid sleep deprivation. The authors reported that based on sleep diary, sleep quality increased in the regular sleep but not sleep only condition; compared to the sleep only group, the regular sleep group had greater and longer lasting reduction in daytime sleepiness.
  3. Shen et al. (2008) experimentally enforced regular sleep timing and other routines in university students with irregular sleep patterns and at risk for bipolar spectrum disorders. Despite significantly greater reduction in sleep duration IIV in the experimental versus control group, increased lifestyle regularity did not result in changes in mood lability or manic/depressive symptoms.

Methods for Quantifying IIV

Following considerations are necessary When choosing analytic method for sleep IIV:

  1. Does it account for systematic trends? Methods that do not account for systemic trends (e.g., therapeutic effects of a sleep intervention, effects of seasonal variation in daylight on sleep timing) might inflate the estimation of IIV if such trends are not of direct relevance to the hypothesis.
  2. Does it address measurement error? What's the required sample size and number of repeated measures to achieve reliable measure of IIV?
  3. Does it address missing data? Missing data is common in daily measures. Analytic approaches that address missing data are more appropriate than listwise deletion or omission.
  4. What are the covariates to include? We recommend that the individual mean of the sleep variable be included as a covariate when the effects of the IIV are examined. This helps assess the distinctive effects of IIV beyond the mean values. This is particularly recommended for sleep variables with skewed distribution (e.g., SOL), such that individuals with more extreme means have smaller IIV due to floor and ceiling effects. Compared to variables that are typically normally distributed (e.g., sleep duration and timing), skewed variables are more likely to be affected by this effect as correlations between the individual mean and IIV are likely to be higher in these variables.

Formula Based Calculations

The individual standard deviation (ISD) and coefficient of variation (CV) are the most commonly used methods by existing studies. Others include, variance, range, interquartile range, mean successive squared difference (MSSD), and root mean squared deviation (RMSD).

Limitations:
  1. All above mentioned approaches do not account for measurement error and are susceptible to low reliability, particularly when the number of repeated observations and/or individual differences in IIV is small?. Unlike means, which have good reliability with a few repeated measures, if IIV were to be estimated using ISD, as many as 50 repeated observations would be required for a reliable instrument (reliability .9) to achieve reasonable reliability (reliability .8)?.
  2. These methods do not address missing data. This can lead to reduction in usable sample sizes (in cases of listwise deletion) and biases towards days with no missing data (in cases of omission).
  3. ISD, variance, range, and CV, may inflate the estimation of IIV by including systematic time effects. Methods such as MSSD are less sensitive to such effects, but have significant limitations when missing data is present.

Bayesian Variability Model (BVM)

A flexible Bayesian method for analyzing was developed by Wiley et al. An open access pre-print of the method can be accessed below. The BVM can be thought of having two components:

  1. A measurement model that estimates intraindividual variability (IIV)
  2. A substantive model that predicts an outcome from IIV and other predictors as desired. Often, the intraindividual mean (IIM) is included at a minimum to control for the fact that the IIV and IIM are commonly correlated, especially for sleep variables such as sleep onset latency.

The key advance of the BVM is that by simultaneously estimating IIV with the substantive model of interest, it can take into account the measurement error of IIV. For IIM, measurement error may be modest, but measurement for IIV is substantial unless many observations are available. Practically, the BVM can be considered a special case of Bayesian, location-scale, multilevel, structural equation models in that includes both a measurement model and structural model (IIV predicting the outcome of interest).

The BVM differs from existing methods for modeling IIV in accounting for measurement error and using IIV as a predictor, rather than an outcome to be predicted, such as for Hedeker's Location-Scale Mixed Model. Simulation studies show that the BVM produces unbiased estimates for the relationship between IIV and an outcome as well as good coverage, meaning the 95% credible intervals include the true value about 95% of the time, with as few as five repeated measures used to assess IIV. In contrast using an individual standard deviation and ignoring measurement error yields substantial bias in the point estimates and inaccurate confidence intervals over or under estimating the true population relationship.

The BVM uses freely available, open-source, scientific software. The user interface is written in R and Stan is used for the actual Bayesian estimation. One challenge to the BVM is that due to simultaneously estimating IIV and the substantive model, models tend to be computationally intensive and may require several minutes to complete for small datasets (e.g., 100 participants, 7-14 repeated measures per participant), and longer for larger datasets. A modern, desktop computer with at least 2-4 processors or access to a server is recommended.

Wiley, Bei, Trinder, & Manber (2014). Variability as a Predictor: A Bayesian Variability Model for small samples and few repeated measures. arXiv preprint arXiv:1411.2961.

This method is accessible via a free open source R package, varian. It can be access via GitHub or CRAN.

Regularity Index

The Sleep Regularity Index (SRI) is calculated as the percentage probability of an individual being in the same state (sleep vs. awake) at any 2 time points, 24h apart, averaged across the observation period. The index is scaled so that an individual who sleeps and wakes at exactly the same times each day scores 100, whereas an individual who sleeps and wakes at random scores 0. Like other formula based IIV approaches, the SRI is easy to use, and can be incorporated into analyses like an additional variable. A further advantage is that it is easily interpretable with 0-100 scale. However, it does have limitations in handling missing data, and does not model systemic trend.
